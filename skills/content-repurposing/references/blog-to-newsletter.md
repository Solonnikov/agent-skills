# Blog → Newsletter

Newsletters are personal. They read like the author writing directly to the subscriber. Adapt the source accordingly.

## Two newsletter modes

### Mode 1: Same content, delivered

Use when the piece is substantial and the newsletter's value is delivery + curation. The email body is effectively the whole post, maybe with a short intro.

Structure:

```
Hey,

[One short personal paragraph — what prompted you to write this. Context, not content.]

---

[Full or near-full post content.]

---

If this was useful, forward it. If you're seeing it for the first time — subscribe here: [link].
```

### Mode 2: Short blurb + link

Use when the post is a detailed technical piece, or when newsletter subscribers expect brevity. The newsletter is a nudge to click through.

Structure:

```
Hey,

This week I wrote about [ONE SENTENCE — the hook].

[2–3 paragraphs summarizing the key points. Specific, not vague.]

Full post: [link]

---

Also worth your time:
- [Curated link 1 + one-line why]
- [Curated link 2 + one-line why]
```

Mode 2 is usually better. Email inboxes are crowded; respect the subscriber's time.

## Voice

Newsletter voice is 10–30% warmer than blog voice. Signals that work:

- **Direct address**: "you", "this one's for you if..."
- **First-person**: "I spent 3 hours debugging this last week."
- **Anecdote as framing**: "A subscriber asked me last month about X. Here's the answer."
- **Acknowledgment of the reader**: "I know you're busy — the short version is Y."

Signals that don't:

- "Dear valued subscriber."
- "In today's fast-paced digital landscape..."
- Unnamed aggregations: "Many of you have asked..."
- Corporate voice. You're not a brand; you're a person writing to people.

## The subject line

The newsletter subject line is the most important sentence you'll write. It controls open rate, which controls everything else.

Formats that open:

- **Specific promise**: "How I cut my AWS bill 40% in a week"
- **Question**: "Are you over-engineering your side project?"
- **Story opener**: "The $12k oncall page"
- **Counterintuitive**: "Most prompts are over-engineered"
- **Tight list**: "5 Git mistakes I caught myself making this week"

Avoid:
- "Newsletter #47" — dead subject.
- "This week's update" — generic.
- "Quick update" — signals low value.
- ALL CAPS or excessive emojis — spam filter bait.

Subject under 50 chars; 35 is mobile-friendly.

## Preview text

After the subject, the preview text is what shows in most email clients. Don't waste it.

- First line of the body becomes the preview.
- Use it to continue the hook. "Hey, this week I wrote about X" is a weak preview.
- Better: "Lost 3 hours to a stupid bug last week. Here's what I wish I'd known."

## Structure — the "one thing" newsletter

Modeled on [popular newsletters](https://stratechery.com/) and [Pragmatic Engineer](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/). Works for technical writing.

```
Subject: [Hook — under 50 chars]

Preview text continues the hook.

[Dateline or issue number if you have one]

Hello — this week, [ONE THING].

[Section 1: The thing itself]
3–5 paragraphs.

[Section 2: Why it matters]
1–2 paragraphs.

[Section 3: What to do with it]
1–2 paragraphs.

Full writeup: [link, if there is one]

---

If this was useful, forward it to someone who'd get it.

— [your name]
```

## Structure — digest newsletter

For newsletters that curate + summarize weekly:

```
Subject: [Week's theme — "Week of April 23"]

Hey,

[One paragraph framing — what connects this week's items.]

---

### Section 1: [Topic]
- [Link 1 + one-sentence takeaway.]
- [Link 2 + one-sentence takeaway.]

### Section 2: [Topic]
- [Link 3 + one-sentence takeaway.]
- [Link 4 + one-sentence takeaway.]

---

### Tool / book / idea of the week
[One paragraph on something you're reading, using, or thinking about.]

— [your name]
```

## Length

- **Short** (<500 words): opens get read, not skimmed. Respect of subscriber time is high.
- **Medium** (500–1500 words): most popular sweet spot. Enough to be substantive, short enough to read in 5 min.
- **Long** (1500+ words): reserved for deep-dives your audience specifically signed up for.

Err short unless you have a specific reason.

## The CTA

Every newsletter should have one action the reader can take. Not three.

- Read the full post.
- Reply (to start a conversation).
- Forward (to grow the list).
- Subscribe (if you're previewing to non-subscribers).

One CTA, clearly stated, at the end.

## Signoff

- "— [Your first name]" is almost always right.
- "Until next week, — [name]" if you send on a regular cadence.
- Avoid: "Best," / "Sincerely," — too formal for a newsletter.

## Frequency

- **Weekly** — most common, most sustainable.
- **Biweekly** — fine if the content warrants the longer gap.
- **Monthly** — risks subscribers forgetting you; OK if it's a premium-feeling long form.
- **Daily** — only if it's your full-time thing.

Whatever cadence you pick, hit it consistently. Missed weeks train subscribers to stop opening.

## Templates

Keep a plain-text or Markdown template you fill in each week. Reduces cognitive load, keeps structure consistent, makes subject-line optimization possible because the rest is fixed.
