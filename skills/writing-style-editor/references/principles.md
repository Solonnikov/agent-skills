# Principles

Eight rules of thumb that handle most editing decisions.

## 1. Concrete beats abstract

```
Abstract: "We leverage innovative solutions to drive engagement."
Concrete: "We A/B-test the onboarding flow weekly."
```

When a sentence could be on any company's blog, it's too abstract. Specific nouns and verbs carry information.

## 2. Active verbs beat noun + weak verb

```
Weak: "Our team will make a decision on the proposal."
Strong: "Our team will decide."

Weak: "This provides an explanation of the flow."
Strong: "This explains the flow."

Weak: "We need to have a conversation."
Strong: "We need to talk."
```

"Make", "have", "provide", "give" paired with a noun is usually hiding a verb.

## 3. Cut hedges

Hedges dilute claims:

```
Drafted: "I think maybe we could potentially consider doing this next quarter."
Edited:  "Let's do this next quarter."
```

If you hedge, do it once and deliberately. Stacked hedges ("maybe we might") read as indecision.

## 4. Cut intensifiers

"Very", "really", "extremely", "absolutely" mostly weaken the word they precede.

```
Weak: "The result was very surprising."
Strong: "The result was surprising." (or: "astonishing", "unexpected")
```

If you need a stronger word, reach for it. Don't stack modifiers.

## 5. Vary sentence length

This sentence is short. The next one is medium-length, carrying a slightly more complex idea. And then you follow with a longer, more developed sentence that lets the reader settle in for a beat before you snap back to the short pattern. Like this.

Uniform sentence length is the surest sign of AI-generated text. Real writers breathe in, breathe out.

## 6. Put the point first

Front-load the key claim. Background and nuance come after.

```
Buried: "Due to various factors including engineering constraints and the upcoming product launch, we have decided not to proceed with the refactor at this time."

Front-loaded: "We're not doing the refactor right now. Engineering is heads-down on the launch."
```

Inverted pyramid — most important thing first — is not just for news.

## 7. Specific examples beat generalities

```
General: "The docs should cover common use cases."
Specific: "The docs should cover: how to authenticate, how to paginate, and how to handle rate limits."
```

A reader remembers three concrete things better than one abstract claim.

## 8. Read it aloud

If you stumble, the reader will. Most overly-long sentences sound fine silently and terrible out loud. The ear catches what the eye skims.

If the writer can't read it aloud, mentally or actually run the rhythm. Fix the stumbles.

## Bonus — one thing NOT to do

**Don't optimize for word count at the expense of meaning.** A shorter sentence that says less isn't better. Tightening removes filler, not substance. If cutting a word changes what the sentence claims, put it back.
