# Subject lines

Subject lines decide whether the email gets opened. Mobile Gmail shows ~35 characters; most people decide in 2 seconds.

## Structures that work

### Specific ask + time/constraint

```
15 min on pricing for Q2? 
Deck for Thursday review
Renewal paperwork — needs your signature by Fri
```

Tells the reader exactly what's inside and when they need to act.

### Question

```
Are you free Tuesday 2pm?
Quick question on the invoice
Did you want option A or B?
```

Questions invite a reply. Don't use if the body contains a different question.

### Reference

```
Re: onboarding call — follow-up
After our chat yesterday
About the Slack thread
```

Signals continuity. Good for follow-ups.

### Prefix tags

When you have a lot of internal email, prefixes help readers triage:

- `[Action needed]` — requires them to do something.
- `[FYI]` — no action required.
- `[Decision]` — decision requested by a specific date.
- `[Question]` — quick answer needed.
- `[URGENT]` — use rarely; abuse kills its power.

Team conventions matter more than universal rules. If your team uses `[Action]` and `[Heads up]`, match that.

## Structures that fail

### Generic

- "Meeting" — which meeting, when, about what?
- "Follow-up" — on what?
- "Question" — many things are questions.
- "Quick question" — makes people suspect it's not quick.

### Clickbait

- "You won't believe what happened..."
- "URGENT URGENT URGENT"
- Subject lines in ALL CAPS.

Kills trust; may hit spam filters.

### Apology as subject

- "Sorry"
- "So sorry about this"
- "My apologies"

Tells the reader nothing. "Missed the deadline — here's what I'm doing" is a better apology subject.

### No subject

Forever the worst. Many email clients won't even show the preview.

## Subject matches body, always

```
Subject: "Quick question on pricing"
Body: "...also I need feedback on the deck... and when can we ship the feature?..."
```

Three things hidden under one subject. At best, only one gets answered. Send three emails with three subjects, or say clearly in the body: "Three things — 1, 2, 3".

## Reply hygiene

### Change the subject when the topic changes

If "Re: Monday planning" becomes "Re: Monday planning" with discussion of a different project, the thread gets lost. Start a new thread with a new subject.

Gmail's "cathedrals on a thread" phenomenon (everyone replying to different parts of one old thread) is a product of never starting new subjects.

### Keep the `Re:` prefix short

`Re: Re: Fwd: Re: Re: [Urgent]` is unreadable. Most clients collapse `Re:` chains, but some don't. Consider trimming manually on very old threads.

### Edit the subject for clarity

If the thread started as "Budget question" but is now about a spreadsheet, change it to "Spreadsheet for budget review". Your future self searching email will thank you.

## Length

Under 50 characters is safe on every client. Under 35 is mobile-friendly. Longer gets truncated.

```
Good (32 chars): "15 min on pricing this week?"
Bad  (64 chars): "Hi — wondering if you might have 15 minutes for a pricing chat?"
```

The shorter one answers more of the reader's "what is this?" in less screen space.
