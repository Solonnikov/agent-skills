# Tone and voice

## The register dial

Email register runs from "formal correspondence" to "text to a friend". Most business email lives in three positions:

| Register | When | Opener | Closer |
|----------|------|--------|--------|
| **Formal** | First email to someone important, legal / HR, regulatory context | "Dear Dr. Smith," | "Sincerely," |
| **Neutral** | Most business email, first contact with peers | "Hi Priya," | "Best," |
| **Casual** | Known colleagues, ongoing threads, within your company | "Priya —" or just dive in | "Thanks," / first name only |

## Match the recipient, not your default

If they write `Hi Yaroslav, hope you're well — just a quick one about...` — reply in their register, not yours. Matching register builds rapport.

If they write `yo, got a sec?` — don't reply with "Dear sir."

Exceptions: power dynamics. If they're more senior and they write casually, you can go neutral. Don't go more formal than they set.

## Escalation on non-response

If your second email hasn't gotten a reply, don't escalate by getting more formal or more urgent. Two moves that work:

1. **Shorten.** One sentence. "Hey, did you see the message from last week? No worries if now's not the time."
2. **Give an easy out.** "If this isn't a priority, a 'try me later' is totally fine."

What doesn't work:
- "Just following up AGAIN."
- "Please advise."
- "Per my previous email." (Passive-aggressive; readers notice.)
- CCing their boss.

Escalating fast burns the relationship faster than waiting.

## When to go warmer

- The reader shared something personal (illness, big life event). Acknowledge briefly before pivoting to business.
- You're asking for a favor.
- You broke something. Apologies deserve human warmth, not transactional language.
- It's the first email in weeks — rebuild rapport briefly.

## When to go more neutral

- The exchange is getting emotional and you want to de-escalate.
- You're declining.
- The topic is sensitive (compensation, performance, legal).
- You're documenting a decision for the record.

## Red flags in tone

### Apologies that aren't apologies

- "I'm sorry you feel that way" — blames the feeling, not the act.
- "If anything I did caused..." — hedges the act itself.
- "I apologize for any inconvenience" — generic; doesn't acknowledge specific harm.

Real apology: "I missed the deadline. That's on me. Here's what I'm doing to fix it."

### Passive aggression signals

- "As previously stated..."
- "Per my previous email..."
- "Just circling back..." (sometimes neutral; sometimes code for annoyance — use with care)
- "Not sure if you saw..."
- "Kindly..." (often comes across as cold)
- "Please advise."

If you find yourself writing these, you're frustrated. Wait 20 minutes, come back, shorten the email, and cut these.

### Status-anxiety signals

- Over-apologizing. "Sorry to bother you, I know you're super busy..."
- Self-deprecation. "I know this is a stupid question, but..."
- Excessive humility. "I'd really appreciate it if you could maybe possibly..."

Ask the question. Respect yourself enough to assume the reader will respect you.

### AI tells

- "I hope this email finds you well." Cut.
- "I wanted to reach out because..." Just start.
- "Please don't hesitate to..." — overused; use "reach out" or "ping me" or just "email me".
- "It's worth noting that..." / "It bears mentioning..." — almost always filler.
- Emojis where none belong.

## Sign-offs

Rough hierarchy from formal to casual:

- "Sincerely,"
- "Regards,"
- "Best regards,"
- "Best,"
- "Thanks,"
- "Cheers,"
- (First name alone)
- (Nothing — only in ongoing casual threads)

Don't use multiple sign-offs in the same thread. Pick one and stick with it.

## Length as tone

A long email signals "I care about this" or "I want to be thorough". A short email signals "this is simple" or "I don't want to waste your time". Use deliberately.

When in doubt, short. You can always send another email; you can't un-send paragraphs.
